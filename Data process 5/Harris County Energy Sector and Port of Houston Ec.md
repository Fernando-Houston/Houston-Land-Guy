# Harris County Energy Sector and Port of Houston Economic Impact Analysis 2025

Harris County, Texas continues to serve as America's energy capital while simultaneously expanding its role as the nation's leading port complex. The dual economic engines of the energy sector and Port of Houston drive substantial economic activity, supporting millions of jobs and generating hundreds of billions in economic value across both state and national levels. This comprehensive analysis examines the performance and impact of both sectors through 2025, revealing sustained growth despite global economic headwinds.

## Executive Summary

**Port of Houston** has emerged as the dominant force in Harris County's economic landscape, generating **\$439 billion in Texas economic value** and **\$906 billion nationally** in 2025, while supporting 1.54 million jobs statewide[^1][^2][^3]. The energy sector, led by upstream oil and gas operations, employs over 208,200 workers in Texas with consistent growth throughout 2025, adding 7,300 jobs in the first five months of the year[^4][^5]. Combined, these sectors represent nearly 20% of Texas's total GDP and position Harris County as a critical hub for both traditional energy production and emerging clean energy technologies[^6][^7].

## Energy Sector Economic Impact

### Oil and Gas Employment Performance

The **upstream oil and gas sector** has demonstrated remarkable resilience in 2025, with Texas employment reaching 208,200 jobs in May, representing a 2.5% year-over-year increase of 5,000 positions[^4][^5]. This growth trajectory has been consistent throughout the first half of 2025, with the sector adding 2,200 jobs in May alone compared to April[^4]. The five-month cumulative growth of 7,300 jobs (3.6% increase) signals sustained recovery from pandemic-era lows[^4][^5].

Since the COVID-19 low point in September 2020, the Texas upstream oil and gas industry has added 51,200 jobs, representing a 33.7% increase and averaging 875 jobs per month[^4]. These positions offer among the highest wages in Texas, contributing significantly to regional purchasing power and economic multiplier effects[^4].

The broader **energy services sector** employs 635,077 workers across Texas, though this sector experienced slight monthly declines in mid-2025, losing 3,153 positions in June[^8][^9]. This adjustment reflects ongoing global economic uncertainty and shifting policy landscapes, yet the sector maintains its essential role in supporting American energy leadership[^8].

### Petrochemical Industry Performance

The **petrochemical sector** faces significant challenges in 2025, with industry leaders describing the current environment as the worst business downturn in a generation[^10]. Global overcapacity built during the COVID-19 pandemic has created substantial pressure on margins, particularly affecting European operations where major producers are reviewing assets for potential closure[^10].

However, Harris County's petrochemical complex benefits from several competitive advantages. **LyondellBasell's closure** of its 263,776 barrels-per-day Houston refinery in March 2025 represents a significant capacity reduction, but new facilities continue to emerge[^11][^12]. **Pasadena Performance Products**, a new alkylate production facility near Houston owned by Next Wave Energy Partners, began operations, focusing on high-value motor gasoline blending components[^12].

The **40th World Petrochemical Conference** held in Houston in March 2025 brought together over 1,500 industry participants from 53 countries, reinforcing Houston's position as the global petrochemical capital[^10][^13]. Industry discussions centered on navigating the energy transition, securing low-carbon feedstocks, and addressing supply chain challenges[^14][^15].

### Renewable Energy Investments

Harris County has made substantial commitments to renewable energy development in 2025. The county approved a **\$10.1 million Distributed Energy Resources pilot project** to install solar panels, battery storage, and electric vehicle charging stations at 16 community facilities[^16][^17]. Phase I construction began in January 2025, with completion scheduled for the first quarter of 2026[^16].

The **Harris County Climate Justice Plan**, approved in April 2025, establishes a comprehensive framework for climate action combining ecology, infrastructure, economy, community, and culture initiatives[^18][^19]. This plan positions Harris County as the first U.S. county to prepare a resiliency plan covering its entire population rather than just government operations[^19].

Additional renewable energy initiatives include the **Solar for All Program**, which provides discounted rooftop solar installations to low-income households[^20][^21]. The **Switch Together** solar group purchasing program launched in April 2025, leveraging collective buying power to reduce upfront costs and expand clean energy access across Harris County[^20].

**Summit Next Gen's announcement** of a \$1.6 billion sustainable aviation fuel facility represents the largest ethanol-to-jet fuel production facility planned for North America, demonstrating significant private sector investment in clean energy infrastructure[^22].

## Port of Houston Economic Impact

### Cargo Volume and Trade Statistics

Port Houston has maintained strong operational performance throughout 2025, handling **381,640 TEUs in May**, representing a 5% year-over-year increase[^1][^23]. Year-to-date container volumes reached **1.837 million TEUs through May**, up 4% compared to 2024[^1]. The port achieved a record-breaking January with 356,407 TEUs, the highest January container volume in its history[^24].

![Port Houston container volumes showing steady growth through 2025, reaching over 381,000 TEUs in May](https://ppl-ai-code-interpreter-files.s3.amazonaws.com/web/direct-files/f0e707b3bef907c8a68c962a47a02477/6ca0f5a4-d933-4577-8cd2-22cab6b7d084/10e43a5a.png)

**General cargo performance** has been particularly strong, with 12% year-to-date growth, while steel imports increased 11%[^1][^25]. Total tonnage across all Port Houston public facilities reached **22.9 million tons through May**, representing 2% growth year-over-year[^1]. March 2025 marked the busiest March on record with 386,864 TEUs, driven by a 14% increase in loaded exports[^25].

The **Houston Ship Channel complex** processed over **309 million short tons** of cargo in 2023 (most recent complete data), maintaining its position as the nation's largest port for waterborne tonnage[^26]. The tonnage gap between Houston and the second-ranked port increased to 92 million tons, demonstrating Houston's continued dominance[^26].

### International Commerce and Trade Relationships

Houston led the nation in exports in 2024, shipping **\$180.9 billion** in goods and commodities abroad, exceeding the combined value of New York and Los Angeles[^6][^7]. Energy remains central to Houston's trade profile, with oil and refined petroleum products accounting for **52.1% of total trade value**[^6]. The Houston/Galveston Customs District recorded \$196.1 billion in energy trade, with 88% exported overseas[^7].

**Container trade partnerships** show Europe/Mediterranean as the leading region at 24.4% of total volume, followed by Far Asia at 23.6%[^27]. South America represents 20.7% of containerized trade, while India/Middle East accounts for 16.7%[^27]. This diversified trade base provides resilience against regional economic disruptions.

The port's **international passenger traffic** reached record levels with 13.1 million passengers handled by regional airports and 3.4 million loaded container units processed, highlighting Houston's role as a global gateway[^6].

### Logistics Employment and Infrastructure Development

Port Houston's operations support extensive employment throughout the logistics sector. The port directly creates nearly **190,000 new Texas jobs** through its terminal operations[^28]. The broader Houston Ship Channel complex supports **1.54 million jobs** throughout Texas and **3.37 million jobs** nationwide[^1][^3][^28].

**Infrastructure investments** continue to enhance operational capacity. The **Express Pass program**, fully implemented in late 2024, streamlines truck gate operations through appointment scheduling, reducing congestion and improving predictability[^1][^23]. The new **Execution and Visibility Platform (EVP)** provides real-time operational data access through cloud-based APIs, enhancing supply chain transparency[^1][^23].

**Project 11**, the final phase of the Houston Ship Channel Expansion, neared completion in summer 2025[^1][^23]. Segment 1A, encompassing 11.5 miles from Bolivar Road to Redfish, enables unrestricted two-way traffic for larger vessels and reduces transit times by approximately one hour[^28].

## Combined Economic Impact Analysis

### Employment Landscape

Harris County's total nonfarm employment reached **3.471 million jobs** in May 2025, with the region maintaining relative stability despite national economic pressures[^29][^30]. The unemployment rate stood at **4.3% in May 2025**, slightly above the national average of 4.0%[^31][^32].

The **Greater Houston Partnership forecasts** 71,200 new jobs for 2025, with healthcare and construction expected to lead growth[^33][^34]. Energy sector employment is projected to add 2,200 positions despite the industry being 25% smaller than its 2014 peak[^34].

**Trade, Transportation, and Utilities** added 11,862 jobs in Q4 2024, representing 2.4% growth and demonstrating the port's continued employment impact[^31]. Construction employment grew 4.2% in the same period, supported by over \$32.8 billion in construction contracts awarded through October 2024[^34].

### Economic Multiplier Effects

The combined economic impact of energy and port activities creates substantial multiplier effects throughout Harris County. Port Houston generates **\$285,065 in economic value per supported job**, while supporting 3,508 jobs per billion dollars of economic impact.

The port's economic multiplier of 2.06x between national and state impact demonstrates its broad reach beyond regional boundaries.

**Energy sector wages** rank among Texas's highest, with upstream oil and gas positions providing significant purchasing power that supports local businesses and services[^4]. The sector's 1.45% share of total Texas employment understates its economic influence due to these premium wage levels.

### Future Growth Projections

Both sectors face evolving challenges and opportunities in 2025 and beyond. **Energy transition investments** are accelerating, with Houston positioning itself as a leader in hydrogen, carbon capture, and sustainable aviation fuels[^35][^36][^37]. The **Houston Energy Transition Initiative (HETI)** continues expanding its membership and working groups to advance low-carbon energy solutions[^37][^38].

**Port expansion projects** support long-term capacity growth. Construction of Bayport Container Terminal's Wharf 7 continues toward 2026 completion, with Wharf 1 following in 2027[^24]. Additional ship-to-shore cranes and rubber-tired gantry cranes scheduled for 2026 will enhance throughput capacity[^24].

The **Houston Ship Channel's strategic position** as both an energy hub and international gateway provides unique competitive advantages as global trade patterns evolve. Recent supply chain disruptions have increased interest in Gulf Coast ports, potentially benefiting Houston's market position[^7][^28].

## Conclusion

Harris County's dual economic pillars of energy production and port operations continue generating substantial economic value in 2025, supporting millions of jobs and contributing nearly 20% of Texas's GDP. While facing headwinds from global economic uncertainty and energy transition pressures, both sectors demonstrate resilience through strategic investments, infrastructure improvements, and adaptation to evolving market conditions.

The port's record-breaking container volumes, combined with the energy sector's employment growth and renewable energy investments, position Harris County for continued economic leadership. As these sectors navigate transformation toward more sustainable operations, their combined impact ensures Harris County remains a critical driver of both regional prosperity and national economic strength.
